Goat Milk Recipes

From fresh cheese to handmade soap, goat milk is one of the most versatile homestead products you can work with. These beginner-friendly recipes will help you make the most of every gallon.

Why Goat Milk?

Goat milk has been enjoyed around the world for thousands of years, and for good reason. Whether you are drinking it fresh, turning it into cheese, or using it in body care products, goat milk offers some real advantages over cow milk.

  • Easier to digest — Goat milk has smaller fat globules and a softer curd, making it gentler on the stomach for many people.
  • Naturally homogenized — The cream does not separate as readily as in cow milk, giving it a smooth, consistent texture.
  • Less allergenic for some people — Goat milk contains lower levels of alpha-s1 casein, the protein most commonly associated with dairy sensitivities.
  • Versatile — It works beautifully in cooking, baking, cheese making, and body care products like soap and lotion.
  • Rich in nutrients — Goat milk is an excellent source of calcium, potassium, vitamin A, and essential fatty acids.

Tip: For the best-tasting milk and best recipe results, chill your milk quickly after milking and use it within a few days. Clean milking practices make all the difference in flavor.

Simple Chevre (Fresh Goat Cheese)

Chevre is one of the easiest cheeses you can make at home and is the perfect first project for beginners. It has a creamy, tangy flavor that works on everything from crackers to salads.

Ingredients

  • 1 gallon fresh goat milk (pasteurized or raw)
  • 1 packet mesophilic starter culture (or 1/4 teaspoon direct-set)
  • 2 drops liquid rennet diluted in 1/4 cup cool, non-chlorinated water
  • 1 teaspoon cheese salt (non-iodized)

Equipment

  • Large stainless steel pot
  • Thermometer
  • Butter muslin or fine cheesecloth
  • Colander

Instructions

  1. Heat the goat milk in a stainless steel pot to 86°F (30°C), stirring gently.
  2. Sprinkle the mesophilic culture over the surface of the milk. Let it rehydrate for 2 minutes, then stir gently with an up-and-down motion for about 1 minute.
  3. Add the diluted rennet and stir gently for 30 seconds.
  4. Cover the pot and let it sit undisturbed at room temperature for 12 to 18 hours. The milk will set into a soft curd.
  5. Line a colander with butter muslin and place it over a bowl. Gently ladle the curds into the cloth.
  6. Tie the corners of the cloth together and hang the bundle to drain for 6 to 12 hours, depending on how thick you want your cheese. Less time gives a creamier spread; more time gives a firmer log.
  7. Transfer the drained cheese to a bowl, add salt, and mix thoroughly.
  8. Shape into a log, ball, or press into a mold. Refrigerate.

Flavor Variations

  • Herb chevre: Mix in finely chopped fresh chives, dill, thyme, or rosemary.
  • Garlic and pepper: Add roasted garlic and cracked black pepper.
  • Honey and walnut: Drizzle with local honey and top with crushed walnuts for a sweet version.
  • Sun-dried tomato and basil: Fold in chopped sun-dried tomatoes and fresh basil.

Yield: One gallon of goat milk produces approximately 1 pound of chevre. The cheese will keep in the refrigerator for about one week.

Goat Milk Feta

Feta made from goat milk has a bright, tangy flavor that is perfect for salads, pasta, and Mediterranean dishes. It takes a bit more time than chevre but is still very doable for beginners.

Ingredients

  • 1 gallon fresh goat milk
  • 1 packet mesophilic starter culture
  • 1/4 teaspoon lipase powder dissolved in 1/4 cup cool water (optional, for stronger flavor)
  • 1/4 teaspoon liquid rennet diluted in 1/4 cup cool, non-chlorinated water
  • Cheese salt (non-iodized)

Instructions

  1. Heat the milk to 86°F (30°C). Add the culture and lipase (if using). Let ripen for 1 hour.
  2. Add diluted rennet and stir gently for 30 seconds. Cover and let set for 1 hour or until you get a clean break when you insert a knife.
  3. Cut the curd into 1/2-inch cubes. Let the curds rest for 10 minutes.
  4. Gently stir the curds every few minutes for 20 minutes, keeping the temperature at 86°F.
  5. Pour curds into a cheesecloth-lined mold. Press lightly for 4 to 6 hours, flipping every hour.
  6. Remove from the mold and cut the cheese into 1-inch blocks. Sprinkle all sides generously with cheese salt. Place blocks on a mat and let them air-dry at room temperature for 2 to 3 days, flipping twice daily.

Brine Recipe and Aging

Prepare a brine by dissolving 1/3 cup of cheese salt in 1/2 gallon of cool water. Add 1 tablespoon of white vinegar. Place the feta blocks in a container and cover completely with the brine. Refrigerate.

  • Feta can be eaten after 5 days in brine but develops better flavor after 2 to 4 weeks.
  • It will keep in brine in the refrigerator for up to 3 months.
  • For crumbling, remove feta from brine and let it air-dry on a paper towel for 15 minutes before crumbling. Cold feta crumbles more easily than room-temperature feta.

Goat Milk Yogurt

Goat milk yogurt has a naturally thinner texture than cow milk yogurt because of the smaller fat globules, but it is tangy, delicious, and packed with probiotics. Straining it makes a thick Greek-style yogurt.

Ingredients

  • 1/2 gallon fresh goat milk
  • Yogurt starter culture (freeze-dried) or 2 tablespoons of plain yogurt with live active cultures

Instructions

  1. Heat the milk to 180°F (82°C) in a heavy-bottomed pot, stirring frequently to prevent scorching. Hold at this temperature for 5 minutes. This step denatures the whey proteins and helps the yogurt set.
  2. Cool the milk to 110°F (43°C). You can speed this up by placing the pot in an ice bath. Use a thermometer for accuracy.
  3. Add the culture. Sprinkle the freeze-dried starter over the milk and stir gently, or whisk in 2 tablespoons of plain yogurt until fully combined.
  4. Incubate. Pour the milk into clean jars or a yogurt maker. Keep the temperature as close to 110°F as possible for 8 to 12 hours. Longer incubation produces a tangier yogurt.
  5. Refrigerate for at least 4 hours before eating. The yogurt will thicken as it chills.

Culture Options

  • Thermophilic cultures (such as those in store-bought yogurt) need to incubate at 110°F and produce a traditional tangy yogurt.
  • Mesophilic cultures (like viili or filmjolk) culture at room temperature and are easier to maintain but produce a milder, thinner yogurt.
  • Heirloom cultures can be re-cultured indefinitely, saving you money over time.

Greek-Style Straining

For a thick, Greek-style yogurt, line a colander with butter muslin and set it over a bowl. Pour in the chilled yogurt and let it drain in the refrigerator for 4 to 8 hours. The longer you strain, the thicker it gets. Save the whey for baking, smoothies, or feeding to your animals.

Tip: If your goat milk yogurt is too thin, try adding 2 to 4 tablespoons of powdered goat milk before heating, or use a longer incubation time.

Goat Milk Soap (Cold Process)

Goat milk soap is gentle, moisturizing, and a wonderful way to use extra milk. Cold process soap takes patience during the curing phase, but the results are well worth the wait.

Safety Warning: This recipe uses lye (sodium hydroxide), which is a caustic chemical that can cause severe burns. Always wear safety goggles, chemical-resistant gloves, and long sleeves when handling lye. Work in a well-ventilated area. Never add water to lye — always add lye to liquid. Keep vinegar nearby (not to neutralize burns, but to clean up spills). If lye contacts your skin, flush immediately with cool running water for at least 15 minutes. Keep children and pets out of the work area.

Ingredients

  • 10 oz frozen goat milk (freeze into cubes beforehand)
  • 4.2 oz lye (sodium hydroxide) — always weigh by a digital scale, never measure by volume
  • 12 oz coconut oil (provides lather and hardness)
  • 12 oz olive oil (provides moisture and a gentle bar)
  • 6 oz palm oil or lard (provides firmness) — use sustainably sourced palm oil
  • Essential oils for fragrance (optional, added at trace — about 1 oz per pound of oils)
  • Natural colorants (optional — clays, herbs, spices)

Equipment

  • Digital kitchen scale
  • Stainless steel or heat-safe plastic containers (never aluminum)
  • Stick blender (immersion blender)
  • Silicone soap mold
  • Thermometer
  • Safety goggles and chemical-resistant gloves

Basic Steps

  1. Freeze the goat milk into small cubes the night before. This prevents the milk from scorching when the lye is added.
  2. Wearing your safety gear, slowly add the lye to the frozen goat milk cubes in a stainless steel container, stirring continuously. Add very slowly to keep the temperature low. The milk will turn orange — this is normal.
  3. Melt and combine the coconut oil, olive oil, and palm oil in a separate pot. Let them cool to about 90°F to 100°F.
  4. When both the lye-milk mixture and the oils are around the same temperature (90°F to 100°F), slowly pour the lye mixture into the oils through a strainer.
  5. Use a stick blender to blend the mixture until it reaches "trace" — the consistency of thin pudding where drizzled soap leaves a visible trail on the surface.
  6. Add essential oils and colorants at trace, if desired. Blend briefly to incorporate.
  7. Pour the soap into your mold. Tap the mold to release air bubbles.
  8. Insulate lightly with a towel and let the soap sit for 24 to 48 hours before unmolding.
  9. Cut into bars and place on a drying rack in a cool, dry area with good air circulation.
  10. Cure for 4 to 6 weeks. The soap needs this time to fully saponify and harden. Flip the bars weekly for even drying.

Fragrance and Color Options

  • Lavender: Lavender essential oil with dried lavender buds on top.
  • Oatmeal honey: Colloidal oatmeal and a drizzle of honey at trace.
  • Coffee: Replace some of the goat milk with strong brewed coffee and add finely ground coffee for exfoliation.
  • Unscented: Perfect for sensitive skin. The natural goat milk scent fades during curing.

Important: Always run your recipe through a lye calculator (such as SoapCalc) before making soap. Different oils require different amounts of lye, and getting the ratio wrong can result in a bar that is either too harsh or too soft.

Goat Milk Lotion

This simple lotion recipe harnesses the natural moisturizing properties of goat milk. It makes a lovely homemade gift and is gentle enough for most skin types.

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup fresh goat milk
  • 1/4 cup coconut oil
  • 2 tablespoons beeswax pellets
  • 1 tablespoon vitamin E oil (optional, acts as a natural preservative)
  • 10 to 15 drops essential oil of your choice (lavender, chamomile, or tea tree work well)

Instructions

  1. In a double boiler, melt the coconut oil and beeswax together over low heat, stirring occasionally.
  2. Remove from heat and let cool slightly (about 5 minutes) but do not let it solidify.
  3. Warm the goat milk gently to just above room temperature. Do not overheat.
  4. Slowly pour the warm goat milk into the oil and wax mixture while blending with a stick blender or hand mixer. Blend until the mixture is smooth and creamy.
  5. Add the vitamin E oil and essential oils. Blend for another 30 seconds.
  6. Pour into clean jars or lotion containers and let cool completely before sealing.

Shelf Life Considerations

  • Because this lotion contains fresh milk and no commercial preservatives, store it in the refrigerator.
  • Refrigerated lotion will keep for approximately 2 to 3 weeks.
  • Always use clean hands or a small spatula to scoop lotion from the jar to prevent contamination.
  • If the lotion develops an off smell or changes color, discard it.

Tip: For a longer shelf life, you can substitute powdered goat milk for fresh milk. Reconstitute according to package directions before using.

Goat Milk Cajeta (Caramel)

Cajeta is a traditional Mexican caramel made by slowly cooking goat milk with sugar until it becomes thick, golden, and irresistibly sweet. It is wonderful drizzled over ice cream, churros, fruit, or eaten straight from the spoon.

Ingredients

  • 1 quart (4 cups) fresh goat milk
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1 tablespoon vanilla extract (or 1 vanilla bean, split and scraped)
  • 1/4 teaspoon baking soda dissolved in 1 tablespoon water
  • Pinch of cinnamon (optional)

Instructions

  1. Combine the goat milk and sugar in a heavy-bottomed saucepan over medium heat. Stir until the sugar dissolves completely.
  2. Once the mixture begins to simmer, add the baking soda mixture. It will foam up — this is normal. The baking soda helps with browning and prevents the milk from curdling.
  3. Reduce heat to medium-low and let the mixture simmer gently. Stir every 5 to 10 minutes to prevent sticking and scorching on the bottom.
  4. Cook for about 1 to 1.5 hours. The mixture will slowly reduce, darken to a rich golden-amber color, and thicken.
  5. When the cajeta coats the back of a spoon and drizzles in a thick ribbon, remove from heat.
  6. Stir in the vanilla extract and cinnamon, if using.
  7. Let cool slightly before transferring to a clean glass jar. The cajeta will continue to thicken as it cools.

Tip: Cajeta keeps in the refrigerator for up to a month. To reheat, warm gently in a saucepan with a splash of milk or microwave in short intervals, stirring between each.

Goat Milk Ice Cream

Goat milk ice cream has a rich, slightly tangy flavor that pairs beautifully with both fruit and chocolate. The custard method gives the smoothest texture.

Base Recipe (Custard Method)

Ingredients

  • 2 cups fresh goat milk
  • 1 cup goat milk cream (or heavy cream)
  • 3/4 cup sugar
  • 4 egg yolks
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • Pinch of salt

Instructions

  1. Heat the goat milk and cream together in a saucepan over medium heat until it just begins to steam (about 170°F). Do not boil.
  2. In a separate bowl, whisk the egg yolks and sugar together until pale and slightly thick.
  3. Slowly pour about 1/2 cup of the hot milk mixture into the eggs while whisking constantly (this is called tempering and prevents the eggs from scrambling).
  4. Pour the tempered egg mixture back into the saucepan with the remaining milk. Cook over medium-low heat, stirring constantly, until the custard thickens enough to coat the back of a spoon (about 170°F to 175°F).
  5. Remove from heat and stir in the vanilla and salt. Strain through a fine mesh sieve into a clean bowl.
  6. Cover with plastic wrap pressed directly against the surface of the custard (to prevent a skin from forming). Refrigerate for at least 4 hours or overnight.
  7. Churn in your ice cream maker according to the manufacturer's instructions. Transfer to a freezer-safe container and freeze for at least 2 hours before serving.

Flavor Ideas

  • Honey lavender: Replace sugar with 3/4 cup honey and infuse the milk with 2 tablespoons dried culinary lavender (strain before tempering).
  • Chocolate: Add 4 oz melted dark chocolate to the warm custard before chilling.
  • Berry swirl: Swirl in a simple berry compote (cooked and mashed berries with a little sugar) after churning.
  • Cajeta ripple: Drizzle your homemade cajeta into the ice cream as you transfer it to the freezer container.
  • Maple walnut: Replace sugar with pure maple syrup and fold in toasted walnut pieces after churning.

Tip: If you do not have access to goat milk cream, you can use all goat milk (3 cups) with a bit less richness, or substitute cow cream. Adding 1 to 2 tablespoons of vodka or corn syrup to the base before churning helps keep the ice cream scoopable straight from the freezer.

Tips for Success

Whether you are making cheese, yogurt, soap, or a sweet treat, these general tips will help you get the best results from your goat milk.

  • Use fresh milk. Milk that is more than 3 to 4 days old can develop off-flavors that carry through into your finished products. For cheese making, fresher milk also sets more reliably.
  • Temperature matters. Invest in a good thermometer and pay close attention to temperatures in every recipe. A few degrees off can mean the difference between success and failure, especially with cheese and yogurt.
  • Cleanliness is key. Sanitize all equipment before use. For cheese and yogurt, unwanted bacteria can compete with your cultures and produce off-flavors or prevent proper setting. For soap and lotion, clean equipment ensures a safe, uncontaminated product.
  • Avoid ultra-pasteurized milk. Ultra-pasteurized (UHT) milk has been heated to such high temperatures that the proteins are damaged. It will not set properly for cheese or yogurt. Use raw or standard pasteurized milk.
  • Use non-chlorinated water. Chlorine in tap water can kill starter cultures and interfere with rennet. Use filtered, distilled, or spring water when diluting rennet or cultures.
  • Source quality cultures. Reliable suppliers for cheese and yogurt cultures include New England Cheesemaking Supply Company, Cultures for Health, and The Cheese Connection. Purchase from reputable sources and store cultures according to package directions.
  • Keep notes. Write down what you did each time you make a recipe, including temperatures, timing, and results. This helps you troubleshoot problems and replicate your successes.
  • Be patient. Many of these recipes involve waiting — for cultures to work, for cheese to drain, for soap to cure. Rushing the process rarely produces good results.

Tip: If you are new to goat milk recipes, start with chevre or yogurt. They are the most forgiving and require the least specialized equipment. Once you have those down, branch out to feta, soap, and the other recipes on this page.
