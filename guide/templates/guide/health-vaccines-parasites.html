Preventive Health & Parasites

Proactive health management is the key to a thriving herd. Learn to spot problems early and build a strong relationship with a goat-savvy veterinarian.

The Daily Health Check

Spend a few minutes observing your herd every day, especially at feeding time. This is the fastest way to spot an issue. A healthy goat is bright, alert, and has a good appetite.

  • Eyes: Bright and clear, not cloudy or runny.
  • Nose: Clean and dry, no thick or colored discharge.
  • Coat: Smooth and shiny. A rough, dull coat can be a sign of parasites or mineral deficiency.
  • Manure: Should be firm pellets. Clumpy or watery stool (scours) indicates a problem.
  • Movement: Moves easily without limping or stiffness. Stays with the herd.
  • Rumen: The left side should feel full but soft, not tight like a drum (bloat). You should hear gurgling sounds every 20-30 seconds.

Normal Vitals Reference

Knowing what's normal is the first step to spotting what's not. Keep these baseline ranges handy so you can quickly assess whether a goat needs attention.

Vital Normal Range Notes
Temperature 101.5°F – 103.5°F (adults) Kids may run slightly higher
Heart Rate 70–90 bpm (adults) 80–120 bpm for kids
Respiration 15–30 breaths per minute —
Rumen Contractions 1–2 per minute Feel on the left side
Capillary Refill Time Under 2 seconds Press the gum and watch the color return
Tip: Always take baseline vitals on your healthy goats so you know what's normal for YOUR herd. Individual animals can vary, and knowing their personal baseline makes it much easier to spot a problem early.

Core Vaccinations: CDT

The most important vaccine for nearly all goats is CDT, which protects against Clostridium perfringens types C & D (enterotoxemia or "overeating disease") and Tetanus.

  • Kids: Typically receive their first shot at 4-6 weeks of age, with a booster 3-4 weeks later.
  • Adults: Receive an annual booster shot.
  • Pregnant Does: Get a booster 4-6 weeks before their due date. This passes immunity to the kids through the colostrum.
Note: Always consult your veterinarian for the vaccination schedule appropriate for your area and herd. Other vaccines (like for rabies or CL) may be recommended.

Understanding Tetanus Prevention

Tetanus is a deadly disease caused by bacteria found in soil. It's crucial to understand the difference between the two types of protection available.

  • Tetanus Toxoid (Preventative): This is the vaccine included in the CDT shot. It stimulates the goat's body to create its own long-lasting immunity. It is given annually for prevention but takes about two weeks to become effective after a booster.
  • Tetanus Antitoxin (Emergency): This provides immediate, short-term protection by giving the goat pre-made antibodies. It is used in emergencies, such as after a deep wound, castration, or disbudding on a goat that is not up-to-date on its toxoid vaccine. The protection only lasts for about 7-10 days.

Smart Parasite Management

The old method of deworming on a fixed schedule is no longer recommended, as it leads to parasite resistance. Modern strategy focuses on treating only the animals that need it.

Your Toolkit:

  • FAMACHA Scoring: This chart helps you identify anemia (a key sign of the deadly barber pole worm) by checking the color of the goat's lower eyelid mucous membranes. Only deworm animals with pale scores (4s and 5s).
  • Fecal Exams: Your vet can run a fecal test to identify what types of worms are present and in what quantity, helping you choose the right dewormer.
  • Pasture Management: Rotating pastures, avoiding overgrazing, and keeping feed off the ground are the best ways to prevent parasite problems in the first place.

Common Dewormers & Rotation

There are three classes of dewormers available for use in goats. Understanding the classes (not just brand names) is essential for managing resistance effectively.

The Three Dewormer Classes

  • Benzimidazoles: Fenbendazole (SafeGuard) and albendazole (Valbazen). These are often called "white dewormers" and work by disrupting the worm's metabolism.
  • Macrocyclic Lactones: Ivermectin (Ivomec) and moxidectin (Cydectin). These paralyze the worms by affecting their nervous system.
  • Nicotinic Agonists: Levamisole (Prohibit) and morantel (Rumatel). These also cause paralysis in worms but through a different mechanism than the macrocyclic lactones.

Key Deworming Guidelines

  • Give orally: For most dewormers, oral dosing is the most effective route in goats. Topical (pour-on) and injectable forms are generally less effective for internal parasites.
  • Follow up with fecals: Do a fecal egg count 10–14 days after deworming to confirm the product actually worked. This is how you detect resistance early.
  • Rotate classes wisely: Rotate dewormer classes (not just brands) only when resistance develops—not on a routine schedule. Switching too often can speed up resistance.
  • Protect moxidectin: Moxidectin (Cydectin) is often considered the "last resort" dewormer because resistance to the other classes is already widespread. Use it judiciously.
Critical: Most dewormers are used "off-label" in goats, meaning the label dose is for cattle or sheep. Goat doses are typically 2–3x higher. ALWAYS consult your veterinarian for proper dosing. Under-dosing is a major driver of resistance.

Medication Withdrawal Periods

A withdrawal period is the amount of time that must pass after a medication is given before the animal's milk or meat is considered safe for human consumption. Even if you don't sell milk or meat, following withdrawal periods is good practice—and it becomes essential if you ever sell animals to others.

Common Examples

  • Fenbendazole (SafeGuard): No established withdrawal period in goats due to off-label use. Consult your vet.
  • Ivermectin: Typically 35 days for meat withdrawal.
  • Albendazole (Valbazen): Should not be used in the first 30 days of pregnancy due to risk of birth defects.

Staying Current

  • Always check with FARAD (Food Animal Residue Avoidance Databank) or ask your veterinarian for the most current withdrawal times. Rules and recommendations can change.
  • Keep detailed records of all medications given to every animal, including the drug name, dose, date, and route of administration. See our recordkeeping & forms page for printable templates.
Note: Because most goat medications are used off-label, official withdrawal periods may not exist. Your vet and FARAD are your best resources for making safe, informed decisions.
