Meat Goats

Raising goats for meat is one of the fastest-growing segments of small-farm agriculture. Whether you are starting a commercial operation or adding a few head to diversify your homestead, this guide covers the breeds, nutrition, marketing, and economics you need to get started on the right foot.

Popular Meat Breeds

Choosing the right breed is the foundation of a profitable meat-goat operation. Each breed brings a different balance of growth rate, hardiness, and meat quality. Many producers also cross two or more breeds to capture hybrid vigor.

Boer

The Boer is the most widely recognized meat-goat breed in North America, originally developed in South Africa for rapid weight gain and heavy muscling. A mature Boer buck can weigh 250-340 lbs, with does reaching 200-260 lbs. Their distinctive white body with a red-brown head makes them easy to spot. Boers are docile, adapt well to a range of climates, and does are typically excellent mothers that often throw twins or triplets. Kids can reach market weight in as little as 3-5 months with good management. The primary trade-off is that Boers may be more susceptible to internal parasites than some hardier breeds, so a solid deworming program is essential.

Kiko

Developed in New Zealand by crossing feral does with dairy bucks and selecting aggressively for survivability, the Kiko (from the Maori word for "meat") is prized for its parasite resistance, vigor, and low-maintenance kidding. Kikos are slightly leaner than Boers but gain weight efficiently on pasture alone, making them an excellent choice for grass-based operations. Mature does weigh 100-180 lbs and bucks 200-300 lbs. They are outstanding foragers and thrive in rough terrain. Many producers cross Kiko bucks with Boer does to combine the Boer's muscling with the Kiko's hardiness.

Spanish

Spanish goats descend from stock brought to the Americas by early Spanish explorers. Centuries of natural selection have made them extremely hardy, heat-tolerant, and resourceful foragers. They produce leaner, more finely textured meat that is popular in many ethnic markets. Spanish goats are smaller than Boers (does 100-150 lbs, bucks 150-250 lbs) and grow more slowly, but they require minimal inputs and do well on marginal land. Their strong feet and resistance to foot rot are added bonuses in wet climates.

Savanna

Another South African breed, the Savanna was developed from indigenous white goats selected for hardiness, fertility, and muscling. Their solid white coat reflects sunlight, giving them an advantage in hot climates. Savannas are similar in size and growth rate to Boers but tend to be hardier and more parasite-resistant. Does are prolific, maternal, and milk well enough to raise fast-growing kids on pasture. Savannas are an excellent choice for operations in the southern United States and other hot, humid regions.

Myotonic (Fainting Goats)

Myotonic goats, sometimes called "fainting" goats because of a genetic condition that causes their muscles to stiffen momentarily when startled, are smaller than the other breeds listed here (does 80-130 lbs, bucks 130-200 lbs). However, they are heavily muscled for their frame size, producing an exceptionally high meat-to-bone ratio. Their myotonia actually contributes to increased muscle mass. Myotonics are calm, easy to handle, and less prone to escaping fences. They also show good parasite resistance. They grow more slowly than Boers, so they are best suited for smaller-scale or niche operations.

Tip: If you are just starting out, consider purchasing commercial-grade crossbred does rather than expensive registered purebreds. Cross them with a quality registered buck of the breed that matches your goals. This keeps startup costs down while building a productive herd.

Selecting Breeding Stock

The quality of your breeding stock determines the performance of every kid crop. Take time to evaluate animals carefully before buying, and be willing to pay more for proven genetics. A mediocre buck bred to mediocre does will produce mediocre kids year after year.

What to Look For

  • Conformation: Look for a wide, deep chest; long, level topline; thick, muscular hindquarters; and strong, correct legs. The animal should move freely without limping. Avoid goats with narrow chests, steep rumps, or posty hocks.
  • Growth rate: Ask for weaning weights and 90-day or 180-day adjusted weights. Fast-growing parents tend to produce fast-growing kids. If records are unavailable, compare the animal visually to herd mates of the same age.
  • Parasite resistance: Check FAMACHA scores if available. A goat that stays healthy on a minimal deworming schedule is worth far more than one that requires frequent treatment. Ask the seller about their deworming program and how often the animal has been treated.
  • Mothering ability (for does): Does that kid unassisted, clean their kids quickly, allow nursing readily, and produce enough milk to raise twins or triplets without supplementation are the backbone of a profitable operation.
  • Fertility and twinning rate: Select bucks from does with a history of multiple births. Does that consistently single may be less profitable.
  • Structural soundness of feet and legs: Good feet reduce trimming labor and the risk of foot rot. Avoid animals that need frequent hoof attention.

Registration and Performance Records

Registration papers from breed associations (such as ABGA for Boers, NKR for Kikos, or ISGA for Savannas) verify pedigree and can add resale value to breeding stock and show animals. However, registration alone does not guarantee quality. Performance records, including birth weights, weaning weights, number of kids raised per doe, and parasite-treatment history, are far more valuable for making selection decisions.

If you plan to sell breeding stock yourself, maintaining thorough records and participating in performance testing programs will set your animals apart in the marketplace.

Warning: Always quarantine new animals for at least 2-3 weeks before introducing them to your herd. Deworm upon arrival, check for signs of CL (caseous lymphadenitis) abscesses, and test for CAE and Johne's disease if the seller cannot provide recent negative test results.

Feeding for Growth

Nutrition is the single biggest factor you can control to influence growth rates in meat-goat kids. A well-planned feeding program balances forage quality, supplemental grain, and minerals to maximize gain without over-spending on feed.

Creep Feeding Kids

Creep feeding gives kids access to a high-protein supplement (16-18% crude protein) that their dams cannot reach, typically through a small opening in a creep panel. Begin offering creep feed when kids are 1-2 weeks old. Kids will not eat much at first, but early exposure gets their rumens developing sooner. By 4-6 weeks, kids on creep feed can consume 0.5-1.0 lb per day, significantly boosting weaning weights. A simple creep ration can be a commercial goat feed or a mix of whole corn, soybean meal, and a vitamin-mineral premix.

Finishing Diets

After weaning (typically 8-12 weeks), kids destined for market can be moved onto a finishing diet to accelerate growth. A typical finishing ration is 14-16% crude protein with higher energy, fed at 2-3% of body weight per day alongside free-choice hay. Gradually increase grain over 7-10 days to prevent acidosis. Common grains include whole corn, barley, and oats, often supplemented with soybean meal or a commercial protein pellet.

Forage vs. Grain Balance

Goats are browsers by nature and do best when forage makes up at least 50-60% of their diet by dry matter. High-quality pasture or browse alone can produce acceptable growth rates, especially in Kiko and Spanish crosses. However, kids finished on grain will typically reach market weight 4-8 weeks sooner than pasture-only kids. The right balance depends on your feed costs, available pasture, and target market. Buyers paying premium prices for "grass-fed" or "pasture-raised" labels may reward a forage-heavy program.

Target Growth Rates

  • Birth to weaning: 0.3-0.5 lbs/day on dam's milk plus creep feed
  • Post-weaning on pasture: 0.15-0.25 lbs/day
  • Post-weaning on finishing ration: 0.3-0.5 lbs/day
  • Feed conversion (grain-fed): Roughly 5-7 lbs of feed per 1 lb of gain

Tip: Always provide free-choice loose minerals formulated for goats (not sheep or cattle). Meat goats have high copper requirements, and deficiency leads to poor growth, rough coats, and reduced immunity. A quality goat mineral with 1,350-1,800 ppm copper is essential.

Market Weights & Timing

Understanding when and where to sell is just as important as raising a good animal. Timing your kid crop to align with peak demand can significantly increase your price per pound.

Typical Market Weights

Most buyers are looking for kids in the 60-80 lb live-weight range, which most meat-breed kids reach between 4 and 8 months of age depending on breed, genetics, and feeding program. Some ethnic markets prefer lighter kids (30-50 lbs), while others will pay premium prices for heavier animals (80-100+ lbs). Know your buyer's preferences before deciding on a target weight.

Seasonal Demand

Goat meat demand in the United States is strongly seasonal, driven by cultural and religious holidays. Planning your breeding schedule around these peaks can increase your revenue by 20-40% compared to selling at off-peak times.

  • Easter / Passover (March-April): Strong demand for young kids, typically 25-45 lbs live weight. Time breeding for September-October kidding.
  • Eid al-Adha (date shifts yearly): The single largest demand event for goat meat in the US. Buyers often prefer larger, intact bucks. Because the Islamic calendar is lunar, the date moves roughly 10-11 days earlier each year, so plan accordingly.
  • Caribbean and Hispanic holiday season (November-January): Steady demand for roasting-sized goats, particularly in the Northeast and Southeast.
  • Summer grilling season (June-August): Growing demand for goat cuts at farmers' markets and restaurants.

Selling Options

  • Livestock auction: Quickest way to sell, but prices are volatile and you have the least control. Commission and trucking fees reduce your net.
  • Direct to consumer (on-farm or farmers' market): Highest margins, but requires more work in marketing, customer relationships, and often arranging processing.
  • Buying stations and order buyers: Middlemen who purchase for ethnic markets or out-of-state buyers. Prices can be good, especially near holidays.
  • Cooperative marketing: Pooling animals with other producers to fill larger orders, giving access to buyers that require volume.

Tip: Build relationships with buyers well before your kids are ready to sell. Ethnic grocery stores, restaurants, and direct consumers often prefer to buy consistently from a trusted local source and may pay above auction prices for that reliability.

Processing

Getting your goats from the pasture to the plate involves navigating processing logistics and USDA regulations. Understanding the rules before you start selling will keep you legal and help you set realistic prices.

USDA Regulations

In the United States, any meat sold to the public must be processed (slaughtered and butchered) under USDA or equivalent state inspection. This means the animal must be killed in a facility with a USDA inspector present. The resulting meat receives an inspection stamp and can be sold retail, wholesale, at farmers' markets, or to restaurants.

Custom Exempt vs. Inspected Processing

  • USDA-inspected: Required for any meat you sell by the cut or at retail. The meat carries an inspection mark. Slots at inspected plants can be limited, so book well in advance, especially around holidays.
  • Custom exempt: The buyer purchases the live animal from you, then arranges and pays for processing themselves. The meat is stamped "Not for Sale" and is for the buyer's household use only. This is the simplest route legally if selling live animals. Many direct-to-consumer sellers use this model.
  • State-inspected: Some states have their own inspection programs considered equivalent to USDA. Meat processed under state inspection can be sold within that state but not across state lines.

Finding a Processor

Processing capacity for small ruminants is a bottleneck in many regions. Start by contacting your state's meat and poultry inspection office or your local agricultural extension service for a list of USDA-inspected facilities that handle goats. Some cattle-and-hog plants do not process goats, so always confirm. Build a relationship with your processor early and reserve dates as soon as you know your kidding schedule.

Yield and Weights

Understanding yield is critical for pricing your product and setting customer expectations.

  • Live weight: The weight of the animal on the hoof, typically 60-80 lbs for market kids.
  • Hanging (carcass) weight: The weight after slaughter with hide, head, organs, and feet removed. Typically 45-52% of live weight for goats.
  • Take-home (retail) weight: The weight of wrapped, cut meat the customer receives. This is usually 60-70% of hanging weight, or roughly 30-35% of the original live weight, depending on how the carcass is cut and how much bone is included.

Warning: Selling uninspected meat is a federal offense and can result in significant fines. If you are unsure about the rules in your area, contact your state's department of agriculture or your local extension office before selling any processed meat. Selling the live animal and letting the buyer arrange custom processing is always the safest approach.

Economics of Meat Goat Production

Meat goats can be profitable on a small scale, but only with realistic expectations and careful cost tracking. The margins are tighter than many newcomers expect, and profitability depends heavily on controlling feed costs and maximizing the number of kids sold per doe per year.

Cost of Production

Your major expense categories, roughly in order of impact, are:

  • Feed (50-70% of total costs): Hay, pasture maintenance, grain, and minerals. Maximizing the use of good pasture and browse is the single best way to improve profitability.
  • Health (10-15%): Deworming, vaccinations (CDT at minimum), hoof care, and occasional veterinary visits.
  • Labor: Often underestimated by new producers. Kidding season, fence maintenance, feeding, and marketing all take time.
  • Fencing and infrastructure: Goat-proof fencing is not cheap. Budget for ongoing maintenance as well as initial construction.
  • Buck maintenance: Keeping a buck year-round for a short breeding season is expensive per breeding. Leasing or sharing a buck with a neighbor can reduce costs for small herds.
  • Marketing and processing fees: Hauling to auction, processing costs, or farmers' market booth fees all cut into margins.

Pricing

Goat meat prices vary widely by region, season, and sales channel. As a general guide:

  • Auction (live weight): $1.50-$4.00+/lb live weight, highly variable by season and location.
  • Direct sale (whole or half, hanging weight): $6.00-$12.00/lb hanging weight, plus the buyer pays processing.
  • Retail cuts (farmers' market or farm store): $8.00-$18.00+/lb depending on cut, with premium cuts like loin chops at the high end.

Profitability Considerations

  • Kids per doe per year: The most critical metric. A doe that weans 2-3 healthy kids annually is far more profitable than one weaning a single. Select for twinning and good mothering ability.
  • Kid mortality: Every kid lost is lost revenue. Good management during kidding, clean dry shelter, and proper colostrum intake within the first hours of life dramatically reduce losses. Target less than 5% mortality.
  • Feed conversion efficiency: Track how many pounds of feed it takes to produce a pound of gain. Cull animals that grow slowly despite adequate nutrition.
  • Marketing channel: Direct-to-consumer sales can yield 2-3 times the revenue of auction sales. The trade-off is more labor, processing logistics, and customer management.
  • Scale: Most extension economists suggest a minimum of 30-50 does to generate meaningful net income. Smaller herds can still be profitable but typically as part of a diversified farm.

Value-Added Products

Diversifying beyond live animal or meat sales can improve your bottom line:

  • Breeding stock: Selling registered or performance-tested bucks and does to other producers often brings higher per-head returns than selling for meat.
  • Goat jerky and sausage: Value-added processed products require additional licensing and a USDA-inspected co-packer but can command premium prices.
  • Hides and pelts: A small but growing market exists for goat leather.
  • Agritourism: Farm tours, "goat yoga," and educational workshops can generate supplemental income while building your customer base for direct meat sales.

Tip: Keep detailed financial records from day one. Track every expense including feed, vet, fencing, and your own labor at a fair hourly rate. Many producers think they are making money until they sit down and add up the real numbers. A simple spreadsheet comparing income per doe to cost per doe will quickly tell you whether your operation is on the right track.
